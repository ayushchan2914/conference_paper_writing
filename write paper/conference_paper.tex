\begin{abstract}
Electric vehicle (EV) charging station placement on mountainous corridors requires terrain-aware energy modeling, yet most planning methods assume flat-road consumption---an assumption that breaks down on routes with sustained gradients exceeding 1.5\%. This paper formulates a multi-objective optimization for EV charging station (EVCS) siting, sizing, and charger allocation on the 124.8~km Bagdogra--Gangtok corridor in the Eastern Himalayas (1{,}514~m elevation gain), jointly minimizing installation cost, fleet charging detour energy, and customer waiting time. A tractive energy model quantifies per-trip energy consumption, revealing that gradient resistance increases the per-km energy rate by 34.1\%. Charger cost parameters are derived from a survey of five Indian DC charger manufacturers, yielding mean unit costs of \Rs{4.12}{lakh} (30~kW) and \Rs{8.43}{lakh} (60~kW). Three metaheuristic algorithms---NSGA-II, MOPSO, and SPEA2---are compared across 360 independent runs and four charger-capacity configurations. Results show that $N_{\max} = 4$ chargers per node produces a uniquely stable five-station layout with 90.0--96.7\% cross-algorithm consistency and average waiting time near 7.0~min. Charger-rate sensitivity analysis demonstrates that 60~kW chargers enable consolidation from five to three stations with a 15.5\% cost reduction. This framework generalizes to mountainous corridors worldwide where gradient-induced range loss complicates EV infrastructure planning.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Global EV adoption is accelerating, with public charging networks exceeding 3.2~million units worldwide \cite{1}. Yet reliable intercity charging infrastructure remains a critical bottleneck \cite{2}, particularly in developing economies where deployment lags behind vehicle sales. Charging infrastructure availability is among the top three barriers to EV adoption globally \cite{3}, and insufficient grid-connected charger density can create cascading demand problems on distribution networks \cite{4}. Understanding the fundamental principles of EV energy consumption \cite{5} is essential for designing infrastructure that serves both fleet operators and individual drivers. In mountainous regions, these challenges are amplified: sustained gradients exceeding 5\% increase energy consumption by 20--40\% relative to flat-road operation \cite{10,12}, directly reducing effective vehicle range and increasing charging demand at mid-corridor stations. Range anxiety---the fear of depleting battery charge before reaching a charging point---is particularly acute on mountainous corridors where drivers face unpredictable elevation profiles and limited charger density \cite{7,9}.

India exemplifies the infrastructure gap: the FAME India Phase~II program \cite{6} targets 25{,}000 stations nationwide, yet fewer than 5\% of sanctioned public chargers are in hilly districts \cite{7}. Mountainous terrain introduces unique constraints---gradients regularly exceed 8\%, and intercity distances shrink 20--40\% under sustained uphill driving \cite{5,8}---creating acute range anxiety \cite{7,9}. Existing flat-road frameworks \cite{13,14,15,16,17} cannot capture these effects.

The Bagdogra--Gangtok corridor in West Bengal and Sikkim exemplifies the challenge. Spanning 124.8~km along National Highway~10 with 1{,}514~m net elevation gain, this route connects Bagdogra Airport to the state capital Gangtok. Segment~9 (Singtam--Gangtok) alone accounts for 1{,}224~m of elevation gain over 26.6~km at a sustained gradient of 3.88\%---a segment where a Tata Tigor EV consumes 34.1\% more energy per kilometer than on flat terrain \cite{10,11,12}. Similar sustained grades characterize corridors in the Western Ghats, the Alps, and mountainous regions of China and Nepal, making terrain-aware infrastructure planning a global necessity.

Existing EVCS optimization studies \cite{13,14,15,16,17} assume flat-road energy consumption---an assumption fundamentally inaccurate on routes with sustained gradients exceeding 1.5\% \cite{10,11,12}. On steep terrain, gradient resistance dominates aerodynamic drag at typical fleet speeds (30--50~km/h), making the flat-road proxy a poor estimator of charging demand and detour costs \cite{12,18}. GIS-based approaches \cite{13} overlay spatial criteria but cannot jointly optimize sizing and charger allocation. Queueing models \cite{19} capture stochastic demand but fix locations a priori. Multi-objective optimization \cite{14,15,16} generates Pareto-optimal solutions, yet none incorporate terrain-adjusted energy.

This paper addresses that gap with three contributions:
\begin{itemize}
\item A terrain-aware multi-objective formulation in which the energy objective uses gradient-adjusted per-km consumption derived from a tractive effort model, replacing the flat-road proxy used in all prior EVCS work. This is fundamentally different from GIS-based siting studies \cite{13,23,24} that overlay spatial criteria without optimizing charger allocation, and from queueing-only approaches \cite{19} that fix station locations a priori.
\item A systematic comparison of NSGA-II \cite{20}, MOPSO \cite{21}, and SPEA2 \cite{22} with 30 runs each across four charger-capacity configurations, identifying $N_{\max} = 4$ as the design point with highest cross-algorithm layout consensus (90--100\%). Unlike conventional EVCS optimization \cite{14,15,16} that assumes uniform energy consumption, our formulation captures the physical interplay between gradient resistance and charging demand.
\item A charger-rate sensitivity analysis comparing 30~kW and 60~kW configurations, demonstrating that higher charger power enables a 15.5\% cost reduction through station consolidation---a finding with direct implications for corridor planning in mountainous regions worldwide.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{GIS-Based and Queueing Approaches}

GIS methods overlay spatial layers (population, road proximity, grid access) to identify candidate sites \cite{13,23,24}, but evaluate sites independently without optimizing charger count or fleet service quality \cite{25}. Queueing-theoretic approaches model arrivals as Poisson processes via M/M/$c$ queues to compute waiting times \cite{19}, extending to coordinated charging \cite{26} and network impacts \cite{27}. Neither approach alone suffices for integrated planning \cite{28}.

\subsection{Multi-Objective EVCS Optimization}

Multi-objective formulations consider cost, accessibility, and waiting time \cite{14,15,16}, employing metaheuristics---particularly NSGA-II \cite{20}---to generate Pareto-optimal fronts, with MOPSO \cite{21} and SPEA2 \cite{22} offering alternative convergence. Recent work addresses voltage impacts \cite{27}, location strategies \cite{28}, and harmonic distortion \cite{29}. However, all assume flat-road energy. On mountainous corridors, stations must ensure no vehicle segment exceeds range under terrain-adjusted consumption---a constraint not captured by existing formulations \cite{10,11,12}.

\subsection{Terrain-Aware Energy Modeling}

A separate line of work addresses energy consumption under non-flat terrain. Tulpule et al.~\cite{10} quantify road grade impact on EV energy using SAE-standard vehicle dynamics, establishing that gradient resistance can increase per-km consumption by 20--40\% on sustained grades. Guo and Zhou \cite{11} develop GPS-based terrain-aware estimation models, while Wang et al.~\cite{12} extend modeling to hilly terrain scenarios with validated simulation frameworks. These studies collectively demonstrate that gradient effects fundamentally alter per-km energy consumption on mountainous routes---yet none integrate these energy models into EVCS optimization formulations. The few studies that address hilly corridors for EVCS planning \cite{17} use deterministic demand models and lack terrain-aware energy formulations. The present work bridges this gap by incorporating the tractive energy model from \cite{10} into a multi-objective EVCS formulation, enabling jointly optimized siting, sizing, and charger allocation under terrain-adjusted demand.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:formulation}

The decision vector $\mathbf{x} = [x_1, \ldots, x_{10}, y_1, \ldots, y_{10}]$ encodes station status ($x_i \in \{0,1\}$, open/close) and charger allocation ($y_i \in \{0, \ldots, N_{\max}\}$) at each of 10 candidate nodes along the corridor. The formulation simultaneously optimizes three competing objectives:

\textbf{$f_1$---Installation cost} (INR):
\begin{equation}
f_1(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^{10} \left( C_{\text{fix}} \cdot x_i + C_{\text{chr}} \cdot y_i \right)
\label{eq:cost}
\end{equation}
\noindent where $C_{\text{fix}}$ is the fixed site cost (land lease, transformer, civil works) and $C_{\text{chr}}$ is the per-charger cost derived from market survey (Table~\ref{tab:charger_prices}).

\textbf{$f_2$---Fleet charging detour energy} (kWh):
\begin{equation}
f_2(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{j=1}^{N_{\text{ev}}} \sum_{i=1}^{10} x_i \cdot d_{\text{detour}}(j,i) \cdot e_{\text{per\,km}} \cdot \mathbb{1}(\text{EV } j \text{ charges at } i)
\label{eq:energy}
\end{equation}
\noindent where $d_{\text{detour}}(j,i)$ is the additional distance EV~$j$ travels to reach station~$i$ and $e_{\text{per\,km}}$ is the gradient-adjusted energy rate from Section~\ref{sec:energy}.

\textbf{$f_3$---Average customer waiting time} (min): Derived from the M/M/$c$ queueing model in Section~\ref{sec:queueing}.

\textbf{Constraints:} Full coverage requires $\sum_{i=1}^{10} x_i \cdot \mathbb{1}(d_{ij} \leq d_{\max}) \geq 1$ for all nodes~$j$, ensuring every corridor segment has at least one station within the maximum detour distance $d_{\max} = 15$~km. Charger capacity enforces $y_i \leq N_{\max} \cdot x_i$, preventing charger allocation to closed stations. Queue stability requires $\rho_i = \lambda_i / (y_i \cdot \mu) < 1$ at every open station, ensuring finite waiting times.

\subsection{Terrain-Aware Energy Model}
\label{sec:energy}

The tractive effort on segment~$k$ is:
\begin{equation}
F_{\text{TE},k} = C_R M g + M g \sin\theta_k + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho C_D A v^2
\label{eq:tractive}
\end{equation}
\noindent with segment energy $E_k = F_{\text{TE},k} \cdot d_k \cdot (1 - \eta_{\text{reg}} \cdot \mathbb{1}_{\text{downhill}})$. Intuitively, gradient resistance acts like an invisible uphill tax: on a 4\% grade, the $Mg\sin\theta_k$ term can exceed aerodynamic drag by an order of magnitude at typical fleet speeds.

Under flat-road assumptions ($\theta_k = 0$), the energy rate is $e_{\text{flat}} = 0.126$~kWh/km. Under terrain-aware assumptions, the gradient contribution on segment~$k$ is $\Delta E_k = Mg\sin\theta_k \cdot d_k$. The total per-trip energy becomes $E_{\text{terrain}} = E_{\text{flat}} + E_{\text{grad}} - E_{\text{reg}}$, where $E_{\text{reg}}$ is regenerative braking recovery on downhill segments. On the steepest corridor segment (Segment~9, gradient 3.88\%), the gradient resistance term $Mg\sin\theta_k$ exceeds aerodynamic drag by an order of magnitude at the fleet's operating speed---making the flat-road proxy fundamentally inadequate for mountainous corridor planning.

\subsection{Demand and Queueing Model}
\label{sec:queueing}

Demand derives from scheduled airport-transfer fleet movements between Gangtok and Bagdogra Airport. The total mean trip energy demand per vehicle is 20.8~kWh under terrain-aware consumption (Section~\ref{sec:terrain_impact}), and each charging session is modelled as a fixed 15-minute top-up window. Charging demand at each station follows an M/M/$c$ queue \cite{19}, where $c = y_i$ is the number of chargers at station~$i$. Because each EV occupies a charger for a maximum 15-minute window, the mean service rate is $\mu = 4$~EVs/h per charger. The throughput-weighted average waiting time $W_q^{\text{avg}}$ is computed from the standard M/M/$c$ formula and used as objective~$f_3$. This formulation captures the essential tradeoff: more chargers reduce waiting time but increase installation cost, while station placement affects both fleet detour distance and per-station arrival rates.

\subsection{Multi-Objective Optimization Setup}

NSGA-II \cite{20}, MOPSO \cite{21}, and SPEA2 \cite{22} are implemented in PlatEMO \cite{30} with population $N = 200$, generations $T = 500$ (100,000 function evaluations per run), and 30 independent runs per configuration. These three algorithms represent fundamentally different optimization paradigms: NSGA-II uses non-dominated sorting with crowding distance, MOPSO employs particle swarm operators with external archive, and SPEA2 uses strength Pareto-based fitness with $k$-nearest neighbor density estimation. Their agreement on optimal layouts provides strong evidence of problem-inherent structural consensus. Performance is measured by normalized hypervolume (HV, reference point $(1,1,1)$) and layout consistency (percentage of runs producing the modal station configuration). The entropy-MADM method selects a compromise solution from each Pareto front. Vehicle parameters correspond to a Tata Tigor EV equivalent \cite{31}: total loaded mass $M = 1{,}670$~kg, battery 26~kWh, cruising speed $v = 31.2$~km/h, $C_R = 0.015$, $C_D = 0.29$, $A = 2.2~\text{m}^2$, $\rho = 1.225~\text{kg/m}^3$.

Ten candidate nodes were selected at settlements and gradient transition points spaced 6--27~km apart. \section{Impact of Terrain Awareness}
\label{sec:terrain_impact}

Under flat-road assumptions, energy consumption is proportional to distance (0.126~kWh/km baseline). Gradient resistance dominates aerodynamic drag at the fleet speed (31.2~km/h). Mean trip energy rises to 20.8~kWh under terrain-aware modeling (vs.\ 15.7~kWh flat), a 32.5\% increase. Segment~9 alone (3.88\% gradient, 26.6~km) accounts for 4.49~kWh versus 3.35~kWh flat---a 34.1\% segment-level increase showing how a single steep segment dominates the energy budget.

Under the terrain-aware model at $N_{\max} = 4$, the modal layout across all algorithms opens stations at nodes \{1, 4, 5, 6, 10\}. The mid-route stations (nodes 4, 5, and 6) carry the absolute maximum permitted charger capacity ($[4, 4, 4]$), while endpoint stations carry only partial allocations ($[2, 3]$). This structural allocation aligns precisely with the high-gradient zone of the corridor (segments 4--8), where the optimizer physically clusters hardware exactly where vehicles deplete their energy fastest. The spatial correspondence between gradient intensity and charger density provides direct physical evidence that terrain awareness fundamentally reshapes optimal EVCS geometry---a conclusion impossible to reach from flat-road models.

Several key insights emerge from Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity}. First, restricting capacity to $N_{\max} = 4$ forces all algorithms to converge on a single, deterministic five-station layout, confirming that the optimal station geometry is uniquely determined under balanced constraints. Second, waiting time exhibits non-monotone behavior: it achieves a near-minimum at $N_{\max} = 4$ but paradoxically increases at $N_{\max} = 5$ (e.g., 7.03~min to 7.75~min for NSGA-II). This occurs because the optimizer exploits the higher capacity to eliminate an entire station, thereby concentrating fleet arrivals and elevating per-station congestion---consistent with queueing theory, where reducing server count while maintaining total capacity increases utilization variability and waiting times. Third, while hypervolume increases monotonically with capacity (0.202 to 0.361 for NSGA-II), the vast majority of improvement occurs between $N_{\max} = 3$ and $4$ (88\% of total HV gain), indicating that marginal infrastructure investments yield sharply diminishing returns beyond this point.

Mid-route stations (nodes 4--6) carry maximum capacity (4 chargers each), corresponding to the high-gradient zone. Endpoint stations (nodes 1, 10) carry 2 and 3 chargers respectively.

\subsection{Layout Consistency Across Algorithms}

The optimal footprint exhibits maximum stability at $N_{\max} = 4$, where all algorithms converge on the exact same layout. When capacity is too constrained ($N_{\max} = 3$), algorithms open suboptimal stations to meet demand. When overly relaxed ($N_{\max} \geq 5$), the widened feasible space admits multiple near-equivalent configurations, collapsing consistency (36.7\% for NSGA-II at $N_{\max} = 6$). Thus $N_{\max} = 4$ is a ``Goldilocks zone'' where the optimizer is forced into uniquely stable geometric consensus---a finding with practical implications for infrastructure standardization.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper formulates a terrain-aware multi-objective EVCS optimization for mountainous corridors, demonstrating that physical geography fundamentally alters infrastructure planning. By integrating a tractive energy model with fleet queueing dynamics on the Bagdogra--Gangtok route, we find that gradient resistance is the dominant driver of charger demand: the 3.88\% gradient on Segment~9 increases per-km energy by 34.1\%, directly elevating mid-corridor charging requirements.

The most significant finding is that multiple fundamentally distinct algorithms (NSGA-II, MOPSO, SPEA2) and classical benchmarks (p-median, MCLP) all converge on the same footprint: a five-station layout at nodes \{1, 4, 5, 6, 10\}. This cross-algorithmic consensus across 360 runs proves optimal locations are determined by corridor geometry and vehicle depletion rates, not algorithmic preference. $N_{\max} = 4$ emerges as a design ``sweet spot'' maximizing layout consistency (90--100\%) while maintaining near-minimum waiting time (7.0~min). The 60~kW analysis further shows that five stations consolidate to three with 15.5\% cost reduction, demonstrating that investment decisions must jointly consider charger power, station count, and terrain-adjusted demand.

\subsection{Policy Implications}

These results have direct implications for corridor planning. The FAME India Phase~II program \cite{6} and similar initiatives in Nepal, Bhutan, and China's Yunnan province mandate charging infrastructure along intercity corridors, yet current siting guidelines ignore terrain-induced energy penalties. A flat-road optimization would under-provision chargers in the critical mid-corridor zone, leading to queue buildup during peak periods. Our framework provides an actionable methodology: (i)~quantify gradient-induced energy penalties using standard vehicle dynamics \cite{10}, (ii)~optimize placement jointly with charger allocation via multi-objective metaheuristics, and (iii)~validate cross-algorithm consistency for robustness. Planners in the Eastern Himalayas, Western Ghats, or Alpine regions should adopt terrain-aware models to avoid systematic under-provision at high-gradient segments. The 60~kW finding is particularly relevant for developing economies where grid constraints favor fewer, higher-power stations.

\subsection{Future Work}

Several extensions would strengthen this framework. Stochastic traffic demand via Monte Carlo simulation would capture seasonal variability. Grid-side analysis (voltage drop, transformer loading) would identify bottlenecks in remote terrain. Integration of renewable energy sources (solar, micro-hydro) at stations would address limited grid capacity in hill states. Uncertainty modeling for vehicle consumption under varying weather would provide robust seasonal solutions. Multi-corridor extensions connecting multiple hill cities would enable regional-scale planning. Finally, real-time dynamic pricing and vehicle-to-grid (V2G) capabilities would extend the framework from static planning to operational optimization.
